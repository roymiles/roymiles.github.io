Knowledge Distillation: A Visual Guide

Knowledge distillation transfers the rich representations learned by a large teacher network into a compact student model — enabling deployment-friendly models without starting from scratch. This guide walks through the three major distillation paradigms with interactive diagrams, and reflects on where the field is heading.

1. Overview — the Teacher–Student Framework

The core idea, introduced by Hinton et al. [1], is elegantly simple: rather than training a small model purely on hard one-hot labels, we also train it to mimic the soft outputs of a large, pre-trained teacher. Soft outputs carry far more information than a binary correct/incorrect signal—a teacher that assigns 0.6 probability to “cat” and 0.35 to “lynx” is revealing something about visual similarity that a ground-truth label never could.

Key insight: Knowledge is not just what the teacher predicts, but how confident it is across all classes, which intermediate features it builds, and how those features relate to each other.

Modern surveys [2] organise distillation methods into three families based on what knowledge is transferred:

  • Response-based — match the teacher’s final output layer.
  • Feature-based — match internal representations at one or more hidden layers.
  • Relation-based — match the relationships between samples or layers.

All three can be combined, and each adds a different inductive bias to the student. The diagram below gives a high-level picture; click any block to jump to its detailed section.

3. Response-Based Distillation

Response-based distillation is the original formulation [1]. The teacher’s output logits are used as soft targets via a temperature \(T\) that controls how much the distribution is smoothed:

$$p_i(T) = \frac{\exp(z_i / T)}{\sum_j \exp(z_j / T)}$$

At \(T=1\) this is the standard softmax. Larger \(T\) produces a softer distribution, exposing the relative ordering of all classes. The training loss combines standard cross-entropy with a KL-divergence distillation term:

$$\mathcal{L} = (1 - \alpha)\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}(y,\, p_S) \;+\; \alpha\, T^2 \cdot \mathrm{KL}\!\left(p_T(T) \,\|\, p_S(T)\right)$$

The \(T^2\) factor compensates for the smaller gradient magnitudes at high temperatures. Common choices: \(T \in \{3, 5, 10\}\), \(\alpha \in \{0.5, 0.9\}\).

Why not just use one-hot labels? Soft labels encode dark knowledge — a teacher assigning non-trivial probability to visually similar classes is communicating semantic structure for free, acting as implicit data augmentation.

Variants

  • Born-Again Networks (BAN) [3] — iteratively re-train a model of the same architecture using the previous generation as teacher; successive generations consistently improve.
  • Self-distillation — uses shallower sub-networks within the same model as the teacher, requiring no separate training run.
  • Label smoothing — can be viewed as response distillation with a uniform teacher.

4. Feature-Based Distillation

Rather than only matching the final output, feature-based (or hint-based) distillation aligns intermediate hidden representations. Intermediate features capture hierarchical structure — edges, textures, object parts — that is compressed away by the time it reaches the logit layer.

The challenge: teacher and student usually have different widths. You cannot directly compute a loss between a \(\mathbb{R}^{C_T \times H \times W}\) teacher tensor and a \(\mathbb{R}^{C_S \times H \times W}\) student tensor when \(C_T \neq C_S\). This is solved by a lightweight projector.

The Projector / Adapter — a deeper look

A projector is a small module (often a single \(1{\times}1\) convolution or a two-layer MLP) that maps the student’s feature tensor into the teacher’s dimensionality so a direct comparison can be made:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{feat}} = \left\| f_T - \varphi(f_S) \right\|_2^2$$

The projector \(\varphi\) is trained jointly with the student and discarded at inference time — it adds zero cost to the deployed model.

Author’s Work

Understanding the Role of the Projector in Knowledge Distillation

Roy Miles & Krystian Mikolajczyk — AAAI 2024

This paper provides the first principled theoretical account of why projectors work so well. The key finding: the projector implicitly encodes information about past training examples, which in turn enables relational gradients for the student — the student is effectively learning from pairwise relationships encoded in the projector’s running statistics, not just the current batch.

The paper also shows that representation normalisation is tightly coupled with projector training dynamics, and proposes a soft maximum function to handle capacity gaps between teacher and student. The resulting recipe matches state-of-the-art performance at significantly lower computational cost across ImageNet, CIFAR-100, COCO detection, and data-efficient ViT training.

  • BN + ReLU + 1×1 conv — adds capacity and handles distribution shift.
  • MLP (flattened) — used when distilling global features (e.g. ViT [CLS] tokens).

FitNets & Attention Transfer

FitNets [4] was among the first to show that aligning mid-network features outperforms pure response distillation, especially when the student is much thinner than the teacher. They introduce a two-stage training procedure: first pre-train the student to mimic a chosen “hint” layer via a projector, then fine-tune end-to-end with both task and distillation losses.

Attention Transfer [5] aligns spatial attention maps — the sum of squared activations across channels, normalised to unit norm:

$$A(F) = \sum_c |F_c|^2 \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{H \times W}$$ $$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{AT}} = \sum_l \left\| \frac{A(F_T^l)}{\|A(F_T^l)\|} - \frac{A(F_S^l)}{\|A(F_S^l)\|} \right\|_2^2$$

This is cheaper than full feature alignment and often more transferable across architectures.

5. Relation-Based Distillation

Relation-based methods shift focus from individual feature vectors to the structure they form. Even if the student cannot replicate the teacher’s exact feature values, it can learn to reproduce the pairwise or higher-order relationships among samples in the embedding space.

Figure 3 — Relation-based distillation aligns the graph structure of the teacher’s embedding space (purple) with the student’s (green), rather than individual vectors.

Prominent Methods

  • RKD [6] — Matches pairwise distance and angle relationships between example embeddings, transferring relational structure even across very different architectures.
  • CRD [7] — Frames distillation as a contrastive learning problem: teacher and student embeddings of the same sample should be similar, while different samples should be far apart. Strong results with a cross-architecture contrastive objective.
  • NST [8] — Matches the distribution of neuron activations using maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) rather than direct alignment.

6. Language Modeling vs Vision

Although the core taxonomy above applies broadly, the practical instantiation of knowledge distillation differs substantially between vision and language modeling. These differences trace back to fundamental architectural and data-distribution choices.

Output space

In classification, the output is a distribution over a fixed, small number of classes (\(\lesssim 1000\) for ImageNet). In language modeling the output is a distribution over a vocabulary of 30k–100k+ tokens. This makes response-based distillation even more important for language models: the full soft-label distribution is extraordinarily rich, and hard labels (the single correct next token) discard almost all of it. DistilBERT [9] and MiniLM [10] exploit this by distilling directly on softmax logits over the entire vocabulary.

Feature alignment challenges

In vision models, intermediate feature maps are spatial tensors (\(C \times H \times W\)) and the projector can be a simple \(1{\times}1\) convolution. In transformer-based language models the hidden states are sequence tensors (\(L \times D\)) where \(L\) is sequence length. Aligning these is harder because:

  • Teacher and student may have different numbers of layers, requiring a layer-mapping strategy (e.g. every other layer, uniform spacing, or learned mappings).
  • Attention head alignment is non-trivial — heads are not semantically ordered, and teacher/student may have different numbers of heads (TinyBERT [11] distils attention matrices directly, using a linear projector to bridge head-count mismatches).
  • Sequence lengths are dynamic and change per batch, making fixed spatial projectors inapplicable.

Vocabulary and tokeniser mismatch

When teacher and student use different tokenisers (a common scenario in cross-model LLM distillation), token-level response distillation is not directly possible. Recent work addresses this via vocabulary projection — learning a mapping between vocabulary spaces — or by working at the sentence embedding level rather than the token level.

Auto-regressive generation

For generative language models, distillation must account for exposure bias: the student generates its own context at inference, but during training it sees the teacher’s (or ground truth) context. Methods like sequence-level KD [12] address this by having the teacher generate full sequences that then serve as training targets for the student — a form of data augmentation that does not exist in classification.

Diffusion language models

An emerging frontier: applying distillation to masked diffusion language models (e.g. MDLM, LLaDA). Here the teacher’s “output” is not a single forward pass but a full denoising trajectory, and the student must learn to mimic either individual denoising steps or the resulting marginal distributions. Unlike autoregressive models, there is no natural single soft-label to distil from — the exploration of how to best leverage teacher knowledge at test time for diffusion LMs is ongoing, including work on reward-guided stitching [13].

Key takeaway: Vision distillation benefits most from feature-level alignment of spatial maps with a simple projector. Language distillation benefits most from rich soft-label matching over large vocabularies, with feature alignment requiring careful attention-head and layer-mapping strategies.

7. Loss Functions

Most distillation recipes combine multiple objectives. The total training loss is typically a weighted sum:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{task}} \;+\; \lambda_{\mathrm{resp}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{response}} \;+\; \lambda_{\mathrm{feat}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{feature}} \;+\; \lambda_{\mathrm{rel}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{relation}}$$
Loss Formula Used for
KL divergence \(\mathrm{KL}(p_T \| p_S)\) Response-based; soft label matching
L2 / MSE \(\|f_T - \varphi(f_S)\|_2^2\) Feature-based; direct channel alignment
Cosine loss \(1 - \cos(f_T,\, \varphi(f_S))\) Feature-based; direction-only alignment
Contrastive (NCE) \(-\log \sigma(f_T \cdot f_S / \tau)\) Relation-based (CRD)
Gram / MMD \(\|G_T - G_S\|_F\) Relation-based; distribution matching

Takeaway: Feature-based distillation generally offers the largest absolute improvement but requires the most engineering (layer selection, projector design, potential two-stage training). Combining all three families in a carefully tuned multi-objective loss is the approach used by current state-of-the-art methods such as DKD [14] and the projector recipe of Miles & Mikolajczyk [15].

9. Future Directions

Cross-modal and cross-architecture distillation

Most existing methods assume teacher and student share the same modality and broadly similar architecture. An active frontier is distilling from, say, a large vision-language model into a compact vision-only student — or from a convolutional teacher into a ViT student. Bridging these gaps requires more expressive projectors or shared embedding spaces (e.g. CLIP-based alignment).

Data-free distillation

Standard distillation requires access to the original training data. In many real-world settings (privacy, proprietary datasets) this is not possible. Data-free methods generate synthetic “proxy data” by inverting the teacher’s activations, but the quality gap versus data-available methods remains significant and is an open problem.

Automated distillation recipe search

The choice of which layers to align, which loss to use, and how to weight them is currently hand-tuned per task. Neural Architecture Search (NAS) ideas are being applied to this problem — jointly searching student architecture and distillation strategy — but scalable solutions for large models remain elusive.

Distillation for generative models

Distilling large diffusion models (image and language) is an increasingly important problem. Score distillation sampling (SDS) and consistency distillation have shown that a student can learn to generate in far fewer denoising steps than the teacher, but theoretical understanding of why and when this works is still developing.

Theory of projectors and representation alignment

Following Miles & Mikolajczyk [15], there is growing interest in understanding what information a projector encodes and why aligning normalised representations is more stable than raw feature alignment. Connecting these insights to the PAC-learning and information-theoretic analyses of distillation is an open theoretical problem.

Distillation at scale

For billion-parameter language models, even a single forward pass of the teacher at training time is expensive. Techniques like speculative decoding, layer dropping during distillation, and offline teacher caching (pre-computing and storing teacher outputs) are increasingly important for making distillation tractable at LLM scale.
