Knowledge Distillation: A Visual Guide

Knowledge distillation transfers the rich representations learned by a large teacher network into a compact student model — enabling deployment-friendly models without starting from scratch. This guide walks through the three major distillation paradigms, each with an interactive diagram.

1. Overview — the Teacher–Student Framework

The core idea, introduced by Hinton et al. (2015), is elegantly simple: rather than training a small model purely on hard one-hot labels, we also train it to mimic the soft outputs of a large, pre-trained teacher. Soft outputs carry far more information than a binary correct/incorrect signal—a teacher that assigns 0.6 probability to “cat” and 0.35 to “lynx” is revealing something about visual similarity that a ground-truth label never could.

Key insight: Knowledge is not just what the teacher predicts, but how confident it is across all classes, which intermediate features it builds, and how those features relate to each other.

Modern surveys (e.g. Wang & Yoon, 2023) organise distillation methods into three families based on what knowledge is transferred:

  • Response-based — match the teacher’s final output layer.
  • Feature-based — match internal representations at one or more hidden layers.
  • Relation-based — match the relationships between samples or layers.

All three can be combined, and each adds a different inductive bias to the student. The diagram below gives a high-level picture; click any block to jump to its detailed section.

3. Response-Based Distillation

Response-based distillation is the original formulation from Hinton et al. The teacher’s output logits (pre-softmax scores) are used as soft targets for the student. A temperature hyperparameter T is used to soften the distribution so that the student can learn from the relative ordering of all class scores, not just the top-1 prediction.

pi(T) = exp(zi/T) / ∑j exp(zj/T)

At temperature T = 1 this is the standard softmax. At T > 1 the distribution is smoother, revealing more structure across classes. The distillation loss is the KL divergence between teacher and student soft probabilities, added to the standard cross-entropy loss with true labels:

L = (1 − α) · LCE(y, pS)  +  α · T² · KL(pT(T) ‖ pS(T))

The T² factor compensates for the smaller gradient magnitude at high temperatures. Common choices: T ∈ [3, 5, 10], α ∈ [0.5, 0.9].

Why not just use one-hot labels? Soft labels encode dark knowledge — a teacher that assigns non-trivial probability to visually similar classes is telling the student about semantic similarity for free, acting as a form of implicit data augmentation.

Variants

  • Born-Again Networks (BAN) — iteratively re-train a model of the same architecture using the previous generation as teacher; successive generations consistently outperform the original.
  • Self-distillation — uses shallower sub-networks within the same model as the teacher, requiring no separate training run.
  • Label smoothing — can be seen as a degenerate case of response-based distillation with a uniform teacher.

4. Feature-Based Distillation

Rather than only looking at the final output, feature-based (or hint-based) distillation aligns intermediate hidden representations. This is particularly powerful because intermediate features capture hierarchical structure — edges, textures, object parts — that is lost by the time it reaches the logit layer.

The challenge: teacher and student usually have different widths (channel dimensions). You cannot directly compute a loss between a 512×H×W teacher tensor and a 128×H×W student tensor. This is solved by a lightweight projector (also called adapter, regressor, or translation layer).

The Projector / Adapter

A projector is a small neural network module (often a single 1×1 convolution or a two-layer MLP) that maps the student’s feature tensor into the same dimensionality as the teacher’s, so a pixel-wise or channel-wise comparison can be made.

Design choices for the projector:
  • 1×1 conv — cheapest, preserves spatial resolution, no non-linearity.
  • BN + ReLU + 1×1 conv — adds capacity, handles distribution shift.
  • MLP (flatten) — used when distilling from global features (e.g. ViT [CLS] tokens).
  • No projector — valid when architectures share the same width (same-arch distillation).

The projector is trained jointly with the student and discarded at inference time — it adds zero cost to the deployed model. Its sole purpose is to act as a bridge during training.

Some methods (e.g. FitNets) place the projector on the student side (student → teacher space), while others (e.g. CRD) place it on the teacher side or use symmetric projectors. The choice can subtly affect what the student is forced to learn.

FitNets & Attention Transfer

FitNets (Romero et al., 2015) was among the first to show that aligning mid-network features outperforms pure response distillation, especially when the student is much thinner than the teacher. They introduce a two-stage training procedure: first pre-train the student to mimic a chosen “hint” layer, then fine-tune end-to-end with both task and distillation losses.

Attention Transfer (Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2017) instead aligns spatial attention maps — the sum of squared feature activations across channels, normalised to unit norm. This is cheaper (a single scalar map per spatial location) and sometimes more transferable because attention maps are more architecture-agnostic than raw feature tensors.

A(F) = ∑c |Fc|2    ∈ ℝH × W     LAT = ∑l ‖ A(FTl) / ‖A(FTl)‖ − A(FSl) / ‖A(FSl)‖ ‖²

5. Relation-Based Distillation

Relation-based methods shift focus from individual feature vectors to the structure they form. The intuition: even if the student cannot replicate the teacher’s exact feature values, it can learn to reproduce the pairwise or higher-order relationships among samples in the embedding space.

Figure 3 — Relation-based distillation aligns the graph structure of the teacher’s embedding space (purple) with the student’s (green), rather than individual vectors.

Prominent Methods

  • RKD (Park et al., 2019) — Relational Knowledge Distillation. Matches pairwise distance and angle relationships between example embeddings. Shown to transfer relational structure even when architectures differ wildly.
  • CRD (Tian et al., 2020) — Contrastive Representation Distillation. Frames distillation as a contrastive learning problem: teacher and student embeddings of the same sample should be similar, while embeddings from different samples should be far apart. Achieves strong results with a cross-architecture contrastive objective.
  • NST (Huang & Wang, 2017) — Neuron Selectivity Transfer. Matches the distribution of neuron activations using maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) rather than direct alignment.

6. Loss Functions

Most distillation recipes combine multiple objectives. The total training loss is typically a weighted sum:

Ltotal = Ltask  +  λresp Lresponse  +  λfeat Lfeature  +  λrel Lrelation
Loss Formula Used for
KL divergence KL(pT ‖ pS) Response-based; soft label matching
L2 / MSE ‖fT − φ(fS)‖² Feature-based; direct channel alignment
Cosine loss 1 − cos(fT, φ(fS)) Feature-based; direction-only alignment
Contrastive (NCE) −log σ(fT·fS/τ) Relation-based (CRD)
Gram / MMD ‖GT − GS‖F Relation-based; style / distribution matching

Choosing the right combination and weighting is often empirical. A useful heuristic: start with response distillation (KL) as a strong baseline, then add a single feature-based loss at the penultimate layer. Relation-based losses tend to help most when the student architecture differs significantly from the teacher.

7. Method Comparison

Paradigm What is transferred Architecture constraint Key hyperparameter Typical gain
Response-based Soft output logits None — only need same # classes Temperature T +0.5–2% top-1
Feature-based Hidden layer tensors Need spatial/channel alignment (via projector) Layer selection, projector design +1–3% top-1
Relation-based Pairwise/batch structure None — works across any architectures Batch size, contrastive temperature τ +0.5–2% top-1
Takeaway: Feature-based distillation generally offers the largest absolute improvement but requires the most engineering (layer selection, projector design, potential two-stage training). Combining all three families in a carefully tuned multi-objective loss is the approach used by current state-of-the-art methods such as DKD and ReviewKD.
